================================================================================
INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

The global cryptocurrency market has undergone a fundamental transformation in recent years. Bitcoin, the world's largest cryptocurrency by market capitalisation, has evolved from a niche digital asset into a financial instrument held by institutional investors and regulated funds. As of early 2026, Bitcoin's market capitalisation oscillates between one and two trillion US dollars, placing it alongside major asset classes in terms of scale. The pivotal moment in this institutional transition came on 10 January 2024, when the United States Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) approved the first spot Bitcoin exchange-traded funds (ETFs). This decision opened direct Bitcoin exposure to pension funds, wealth managers, and retail investors through regulated brokerage accounts, triggering billions of dollars in institutional inflows within days of the approval (Mohamad, 2025; Babalos et al., 2025).

The question of whether Bitcoin is sensitive to macroeconomic conditions is not new, but it has become substantially more contested. Early studies found that cryptocurrency returns were largely orthogonal to traditional macroeconomic and financial factors (Liu and Tsyvinski, 2021; Corbet et al., 2018). Subsequent research documented a gradual convergence, particularly during and after the COVID-19 pandemic, as institutional investors entered the market and equity-Bitcoin correlations rose (Bouri et al., 2022; Nguyen, 2022). The relationship between Bitcoin and monetary policy has attracted particular attention: studies covering the post-2020 period find that Federal Reserve rate decisions exert a statistically significant negative effect on Bitcoin prices (Karau, 2023; Ma et al., 2022), while Federal Reserve balance sheet expansions appear to support cryptocurrency valuations through a liquidity channel (Zhao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021). Nevertheless, evidence for the pre-institutional era is mixed, and it remains unclear whether the arrival of regulated spot ETFs represents a structural turning point in Bitcoin's macro sensitivity or simply a continuation of an earlier trend.

Alongside macro sensitivity, Bitcoin's halving mechanism has traditionally been a distinguishing price driver. Approximately every four years, the block reward paid to Bitcoin miners is halved, creating a programmatic supply shock. Historical evidence across the 2016, 2020, and 2024 halvings documents positive post-halving returns (Fabus et al., 2024; Lashkaripour, 2024). However, Veloso et al. (2025) compare all four halving cycles directly and find that the 2024 event produced substantially smaller and less statistically significant price effects than prior cycles, raising the question of whether institutional adoption has diminished the informational content of the supply event.

This study examines whether the approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024 constitutes a structural break in Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic factors and whether it has altered the explanatory power of the halving cycle for Bitcoin returns. The aim is to quantify the change in the association between Bitcoin weekly log returns and macroeconomic variables across the pre- and post-ETF regimes using a full-sample interaction model with a regime-change dummy and sub-sample robustness checks.

Two hypotheses guide the empirical analysis. H1: after institutionalisation through the spot ETF approval in January 2024, macroeconomic variables have a stronger statistical association with weekly Bitcoin returns than in the pre-ETF period. H2: the explanatory power of the halving cycle for Bitcoin returns has decreased after institutionalisation, as measured by the halving-window coefficient losing significance or magnitude in the post-ETF regime.

To test these hypotheses, this study applies ordinary least squares (OLS) regression with heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent (HAC) Newey-West standard errors to 598 weekly observations spanning July 2014 to March 2026. The primary specification is a full-sample interaction model that includes a PostETF binary dummy and three interaction terms — dFedRate×PostETF, dFedBS×PostETF, and HalvingWindow×PostETF — to test whether regime-specific coefficients differ from their baseline pre-ETF values. A Chow structural break test independently validates the ETF breakpoint date. Sub-sample estimation, a monthly frequency replication, the inclusion of M2 money supply growth as an additional liquidity control, and a multi-cryptocurrency panel regression across five major cryptocurrencies with entity fixed effects further verify the conclusions.

Data are sourced from Yahoo Finance (Bitcoin-USD, S&P 500, VIX, and four additional cryptocurrency price series) and the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) service (effective federal funds rate, Fed total assets, M2 money stock). The sample begins in July 2014, which represents the earliest period for which reliable weekly Bitcoin price data are consistently available, and extends through March 2026, the latest observation available at the time of writing.

The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 1 reviews the theoretical and empirical literature on Bitcoin as an evolving asset class, its sensitivity to macroeconomic factors, halving effects, and the role of institutional adoption via spot ETFs, and derives the research gap that motivates the hypotheses. Chapter 2 describes the data sources, variable construction, model specification, and the diagnostic tests employed to validate the estimation strategy. Chapter 3 presents and interprets the regression results for the primary and robustness specifications. Chapter 4 discusses the findings in relation to existing literature, acknowledges the limitations of the study, and draws conclusions regarding the structural role of the ETF approval in Bitcoin's macrofinancial integration.

CHAPTER 1. THEORETICAL AND EMPIRICAL BACKGROUND
================================================================================

This chapter reviews the existing literature relevant to the research questions and develops the theoretical foundation for the two hypotheses. The review is organised into four substantive sections. Section 1.1 traces Bitcoin's evolution as an asset class. Section 1.2 surveys the evidence on Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic factors. Section 1.3 examines the literature on Bitcoin halving effects. Section 1.4 reviews the institutional adoption literature centred on the spot ETF approval. Section 1.5 synthesises the reviewed evidence and formally states the research gap.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 Bitcoin as an Evolving Asset Class
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bitcoin was introduced in 2008 by the pseudonymous author Satoshi Nakamoto as a decentralised peer-to-peer electronic cash system, with the genesis block mined in January 2009. For much of its first decade, Bitcoin functioned primarily as a speculative instrument with a narrow base of cryptographic enthusiasts and early adopters, and its market was largely isolated from mainstream financial systems. The seminal study by Corbet et al. (2018) is representative of this early period: examining the dynamic relationships between cryptocurrencies and traditional financial assets from 2013 to 2018, the authors document that Bitcoin exhibited relatively low interconnectedness with equities and gold, positioning it as a distinct asset class whose price dynamics operated independently of conventional financial markets. Consistent with this finding, Liu and Tsyvinski (2021), in one of the most rigorous early assessments of cryptocurrency risk, demonstrate that Bitcoin returns had no statistically significant exposure to standard macroeconomic or stock-market factors across the period 2014 to 2020. Their analysis covers a wide range of standard risk factors — including the market excess return, size, value, and momentum premia — and finds negligible systematic exposure, concluding that Bitcoin constituted a standalone asset whose returns were primarily explained by its own time-series momentum.

Over the following years, this picture changed considerably. Bouri et al. (2022), using wavelet-based decomposition of high-order co-movement moments in the time-frequency domain, document that Bitcoin–S&P 500 co-movement was very weak before 2018 but strengthened noticeably thereafter. Nguyen (2022), employing a VAR(1)-GARCH(1,1) approach on data through 2022, finds that the S&P 500 significantly influenced Bitcoin returns during the COVID-19 period, indicating that financial stress regimes activate the equity–Bitcoin linkage in ways that were absent in calmer periods. By 2022, the previously celebrated diversification benefits of Bitcoin appeared to erode precisely when investors needed them most (Bakry et al., 2021). Wu (2025), using rolling correlation analysis, documents that Bitcoin–S&P 500 and Bitcoin–Nasdaq correlations peaked at approximately 0.87 in 2024 following major institutional milestones, reaching levels that place Bitcoin closer to a high-beta equity than a diversifying alternative asset. Marti and Reimat (2025) further report that a one percent rise in the MSCI World index was associated with a 4.8 percent rise in Bitcoin by 2025, providing additional evidence of deep macro integration in the most recent sub-period.

The debate over Bitcoin's market efficiency is directly relevant to understanding this trajectory. Yi et al. (2023), examining Bitcoin's price formation over an extended sample, find evidence of weak-form efficiency, implying that publicly known information — including supply-side events such as halving dates, whose timing is fixed years in advance — should be progressively incorporated into prices ahead of the event as the market matures and informational barriers fall. This efficiency argument provides theoretical grounding for the hypothesis that Bitcoin's historically distinctive supply cycle may become less informative over time. The overarching narrative running through the literature is therefore one of transition: from an isolated, speculative instrument to an increasingly macro-sensitive financial instrument, and the present study's hypotheses are formulated against this backdrop.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.2 Bitcoin's Sensitivity to Macroeconomic Factors
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The baseline evidence from the pre-pandemic era is relatively clear regarding Bitcoin's limited macro sensitivity. Lyócsa et al. (2020) study the impact of macroeconomic news, regulation, and hacking events on Bitcoin volatility over the period 2013 to 2019 and find that, while hacking incidents and regulatory announcements significantly affect volatility, scheduled US macroeconomic news announcements produce no meaningful Bitcoin price reactions. Benigno and Rosa (2023), in a Federal Reserve Bank of New York staff report, formally document this "Bitcoin-macro disconnect" in the pre-institutional era, establishing a baseline against which any post-2024 findings should be compared. Their evidence suggests that until approximately 2020, Bitcoin and macroeconomic variables inhabited largely separate informational universes.

The evidence for the post-2020 period is substantially different. Karau (2023), studying the relationship between monetary policy and Bitcoin in an event-study framework around Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meetings, finds that Bitcoin's sensitivity to Federal Reserve policy shocks increased substantially after 2020, with surprise monetary tightening exerting a significant negative effect on Bitcoin prices. The study represents a key empirical precedent for the first hypothesis of the present study, as it demonstrates that the rate sensitivity channel became active over this decade. This conclusion aligns with Ma et al. (2022), who estimate that a one basis point surprise tightening on FOMC days is associated with an approximately minus 0.25 percentage point Bitcoin return — a response magnitude substantially larger than would be expected from a pre-institutional, macro-orthogonal asset. Buthelezi (2025), using Markov-switching GARCH methods on the period 2015 to 2023, confirms that monetary tightening decreases cryptocurrency prices across both high- and low-volatility regimes, reinforcing the conclusion that the interest rate channel became consistently operative across different market states. The international dimension is examined by Elsayed and Sousa (2022), who apply a time-varying parameter VAR to show that G4 central bank policy rates exert spillover effects on Bitcoin, Litecoin, and Ripple, extending the Fed-centric framework to a broader monetary policy context.

The Federal Reserve balance sheet, widely used as a proxy for financial liquidity conditions, provides a second transmission channel. Zhao et al. (2023), applying time-varying parameter VAR to 2017–2023 data, find that quantitative easing exerts a sustained positive impact on Bitcoin prices via a liquidity channel, consistent with the view that asset price inflation during QE periods supports riskier asset classes. Gu et al. (2021) provide event-study evidence around the Federal Reserve's March 2020 unlimited-QE announcement and document significant positive impacts on Bitcoin and Ethereum returns, with the timing suggesting that the liquidity announcement itself was the operative mechanism. The IMF's empirical framework in Che et al. (2023) integrates both channels: Fed tightening is estimated to reduce a common cryptocurrency factor by approximately 0.15 standard deviations — an effect exceeding the equity market response — and the authors explicitly link this amplified sensitivity to the entry of institutional investors into cryptocurrency markets.

Regarding risk sentiment, Choi and Shin (2022) use a structural VAR to disentangle the effects of inflation shocks from financial uncertainty shocks on Bitcoin returns, finding that Bitcoin behaves as an inflation hedge in response to inflationary demand shocks but responds negatively to financial uncertainty as captured by the VIX. This asymmetric response is consistent with the interpretation that Bitcoin functions as a speculative risk asset rather than a safe haven, falling when investor risk aversion rises. Hernandez et al. (2023), employing an autoregressive distributed lag framework, document that economic policy uncertainty negatively affects Bitcoin in the short run, further supporting the relevance of the VIX as a control variable. Lin et al. (2025), using quantile regression on 2018–2024 data, add that the US dollar exchange rate and Treasury yields are significant drivers of Bitcoin returns at multiple quantiles of the return distribution, reinforcing the conclusion that Bitcoin's macro sensitivity became multidimensional over the decade.

Despite this growing body of evidence, a notable gap persists. Most existing studies analyse the evolution of macro sensitivity up to approximately 2023 and do not directly test whether the specific institutional event of the January 2024 spot ETF approval constitutes a measurable amplification of these sensitivities. The interaction model approach employed in the present study, which directly estimates whether post-ETF macro regression coefficients differ statistically from pre-ETF levels, is designed to address this gap.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.3 Bitcoin Halving Effects
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bitcoin's supply schedule is encoded in its protocol: approximately every 210,000 blocks, or roughly four years, the reward paid to miners for validating transactions is halved. This mechanism creates a programmatic reduction in the rate of new Bitcoin supply. The halvings occurred on 28 November 2012, 9 July 2016, 11 May 2020, and 19 April 2024, respectively. The supply-side logic predicts that, holding demand constant, a reduction in the rate of new supply creation should exert upward pressure on prices — a direct application of supply-and-demand theory to a fixed-supply monetary asset.

Empirical evidence on halving effects is broadly supportive of a positive price outcome, at least across the earlier cycles. Fabus et al. (2024) document the post-halving price cycle using technical indicators and regression analysis across the 2012, 2016, and 2020 halvings, identifying a predictable pattern of price appreciation in the months following each supply event. Lashkaripour (2024), using double-selection LASSO and OLS on a multi-cycle dataset, confirms positive post-halving returns while also documenting that the magnitude of halving sensitivity weakened across successive cycles, consistent with the market maturation hypothesis. Liu et al. (2022), providing a comprehensive factor model of cryptocurrency returns, offer theoretical grounding for the diminishing pattern by showing that as Bitcoin matures, its pricing increasingly reflects systematic risk factors rather than idiosyncratic supply-side events.

Two competing interpretations of the halving mechanism are particularly relevant to the present study. The supply-fundamental view holds that the halving exerts genuine fundamental price pressure via the direct reduction in miner-produced supply entering the market. Alternatively, Liu et al. (2025) document negative cumulative average abnormal returns around halving dates and attribute the price dynamics to attention-driven trading behaviour rather than supply fundamentals, suggesting that halvings produce their effects primarily through investor sentiment and media coverage. Under this attention-driven interpretation, halving effects should diminish in a more liquid, institutionally dominated market where sophisticated participants can arbitrage away predictable patterns in advance. Subawa et al. (2025) reach a similar conclusion using partial least squares structural equation modelling, finding that halvings affect prices via sentiment and regulatory channels, consistent with the non-fundamental mechanism. The efficiency lens provides an integrating explanation: Yi et al. (2023) present evidence that as Bitcoin's market deepens, halvings — whose dates are publicly known years in advance — are increasingly pre-priced into the asset's valuation, reducing the measurable post-event return response.

The most directly relevant evidence for the present study comes from Veloso et al. (2025), who compare cumulative abnormal returns and volatility across all four halving events. Their analysis finds that the 2024 halving produced substantially smaller and less statistically significant price effects than any of the three prior cycles, consistent with both the market maturation hypothesis and the attention-based interpretation. This paper constitutes the strongest direct empirical support for the second hypothesis of the present study.

Despite this evidence, no existing study has tested whether the arrival of large institutional ETF inflows, beginning in January 2024, directly contributed to the weakening of the 2024 halving effect. The near-simultaneous timing of the ETF approval and the April 2024 halving creates an identification challenge that standard single-event analyses cannot resolve. The present study uses a halving-window interaction term with the PostETF dummy to directly assess whether the halving coefficient differs between the pre-ETF and post-ETF institutional regimes.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.4 Institutional Adoption and the Spot Bitcoin ETF
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The institutionalisation of Bitcoin has been a gradual process over the past decade. The launch of Bitcoin futures on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange in December 2017 was the first regulated derivatives product, it allowed institutional hedging and created a new price discovery venue. Boan et al. (2021) show that institutional Bitcoin futures trading influenced Bitcoin-S&P 500 correlations in the period before the spot ETF approval, which establishes a pre-institutional baseline from which the ETF's additional effect can be measured. Throughout the 2017 to 2023 period, the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) offered another way for institutions to get Bitcoin exposure, although with the downside of structural premium and discount volatility relative to its net asset value. The SEC rejected multiple spot ETF applications during this time, citing insufficient market manipulation protections.

The situation changed significantly when the SEC approved eleven spot Bitcoin ETFs on 10 January 2024, including BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) and Fidelity's Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC). This was not just symbolic, it enabled direct, low-cost, regulated Bitcoin exposure for institutional investors and retail savers through standard brokerage accounts. Liu and Yang (2024) describe the ETF approval as raising Bitcoin's market legitimacy and liquidity depth, and they argue that this provides a structural reason for why macro factor exposures would change after the approval. Guliyev and Ahmadova (2025) confirm this using cointegration analysis on January 2024 to May 2025 data, finding that ETF assets under management are cointegrated with Bitcoin spot prices, which indicates that institutional flows have a sustained and statistically significant influence on Bitcoin valuation beyond the immediate announcement window.

Price discovery dynamics changed notably after the approval. Mohamad (2025) finds that the spot ETFs, primarily IBIT, FBTC, and GBTC, dominate Bitcoin price discovery approximately 85 percent of the time following their introduction, with the traditional spot market playing a secondary role. Kia et al. (2025) confirm that after 10 January 2024, the spot market overtook futures-based ETFs as the primary venue for price formation, which represents a qualitative change in how Bitcoin prices are determined. These findings directly support the choice of 10 January 2024 as the breakpoint date for the PostETF dummy variable in this study, as they show that the structural change in price formation lines up exactly with the approval date.

The consequences for Bitcoin's correlation with equities have been documented by several studies. Babalos et al. (2025) find that the ETF introduction increased Bitcoin returns and reduced volatility for both Bitcoin and Ripple, which is consistent with a liquidity-driven improvement in market quality. Wu (2025), using rolling DCC-GARCH analysis, reports that Bitcoin-equity correlations peaked at approximately 0.87 in 2024 following the institutional milestones, a level that suggests substantial co-movement with equities and challenges the traditional view of Bitcoin as an independent asset. Hong et al. (2025), using a Chow test at the January 2024 breakpoint alongside DCC-GARCH estimation, find that Bitcoin's correlation with equities increased significantly post-ETF and that its hedging effectiveness against traditional assets changed as a result. Taken together, these studies support the view that ETF-driven institutional entry has pushed Bitcoin toward behaving like a high-beta equity, which implies that macroeconomic variables that govern equity market cycles should increasingly explain Bitcoin returns in the post-institutional period.

Despite this rich descriptive and event-study literature, a direct test of whether the ETF approval amplified Bitcoin's regression coefficients on standard macroeconomic variables across a continuous long-run weekly dataset spanning both regimes is still missing. This study fills that gap by estimating an interaction OLS model over the full 2014 to 2026 weekly sample, allowing the macro coefficients to shift at the January 2024 ETF date, and testing the statistical significance of that shift.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.5 Synthesis and Research Gap
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The review above establishes three broad patterns in the empirical evidence. First, Bitcoin has progressively moved from being a macro-orthogonal speculative asset to one increasingly integrated with the broader financial system, with the Federal Reserve rate and liquidity channels becoming active particularly after 2020 (Benigno and Rosa, 2023; Karau, 2023; Che et al., 2023). Second, the supply-side halving mechanism, which historically drove positive post-halving returns, shows signs of weakening as Bitcoin's market matures, with the 2024 halving producing the smallest measured price effect across all four cycles (Veloso et al., 2025; Lashkaripour, 2024). Third, the spot ETF approval in January 2024 marks the most significant institutional turning point in Bitcoin's history, fundamentally changing price discovery (Mohamad, 2025; Kia et al., 2025) and equity correlation structure (Wu, 2025; Hong et al., 2025). What the literature does not yet provide, however, is a unified empirical test of whether the ETF approval represents a structural breakpoint for regression coefficients on macroeconomic variables and for the halving window premium, estimated over a single weekly-frequency dataset spanning the full 2014 to 2026 period and covering all four Bitcoin halvings.

This study addresses that gap directly. The contribution is a full-sample weekly OLS interaction model that, for the first time, includes both the January 2024 ETF structural dummy and the April 2024 halving within the same estimation window, making it possible to directly test regime-conditional macro sensitivities. The two hypotheses derived from the literature review are stated formally as follows. H1: after institutionalisation through the spot ETF approval in January 2024, macroeconomic variables have a stronger statistical association with weekly Bitcoin returns than in the pre-ETF period. H2: the explanatory power of the halving cycle for Bitcoin returns has decreased after institutionalisation, as measured by the halving-window coefficient losing significance or magnitude in the post-ETF regime.

This chapter describes the data sources, sample period, variable construction, model specification, and diagnostic procedures used in the empirical analysis. Section 2.1 describes the data sources and sample period. Section 2.2 defines all eleven variables. Section 2.3 presents the model specifications. Section 2.4 documents the diagnostic tests conducted to validate the estimation approach.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 Data Sources and Sample Period
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The dataset consists of weekly Friday-close observations for five underlying data series collected from two publicly available sources. Bitcoin price data (ticker: BTC-USD), S&P 500 index data (ticker: ^GSPC), and the CBOE Volatility Index (ticker: ^VIX) are obtained from Yahoo Finance. The effective federal funds rate (FRED series: DFF) and the Federal Reserve total assets series (FRED series: WALCL), used as a proxy for the Federal Reserve balance sheet, are retrieved from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) service maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. All series are aligned to a Friday-close weekly convention. For the daily FRED series (DFF), the weekly observation is computed as the arithmetic mean of the Monday-through-Friday daily values for the calendar week ending on that Friday. For the WALCL series, which is released each Thursday, the Thursday observation is used as the proxy for the week ending on the subsequent Friday. Two additional FRED and Yahoo Finance series are collected for the robustness specifications described in Section 2.3. The M2 money stock (FRED series: M2SL), reported at monthly frequency, is retrieved and forward-filled to a weekly Friday index before computing log growth rates, this variable serves as an alternative liquidity proxy alongside the Fed balance sheet (Sarker and Wang, 2022). For the multi-cryptocurrency panel robustness check, weekly Friday-close prices for four additional cryptocurrencies, Ethereum (ETH-USD), Ripple (XRP-USD), Litecoin (LTC-USD), and Binance Coin (BNB-USD), are obtained from Yahoo Finance and transformed into log returns using the same procedure as for Bitcoin. The resulting panel is unbalanced: Litecoin data are available from July 2014, while Ethereum, Ripple, and Binance Coin enter the sample in November 2017, reflecting their earlier listing dates on the Yahoo Finance platform.

The full sample spans July 2014 to March 2026, yielding 598 usable weekly observations after removing the first observation, which is consumed by the one-period lag of the Bitcoin return. The start date of July 2014 reflects the earliest period for which reliable, continuous weekly Bitcoin price data are consistently available from Yahoo Finance, data for earlier periods are sparse, subject to significant gaps, and affected by the price-discovery problems of the early exchange infrastructure that characterised 2011 to 2013. The end date of March 2026 represents the latest weekly observation available at the time of analysis. For the sub-sample robustness check in Model 2, the pre-ETF period covers October 2014 through 5 January 2024 (n=484 observations) and the post-ETF period covers 12 January 2024 through 13 March 2026 (n=114 observations).

The choice of weekly frequency is motivated by both theoretical and practical considerations. Weekly data capture the medium-term policy-response dynamics most relevant to the macroeconomic variables of interest while avoiding the microstructure noise present in daily Bitcoin data, where bid-ask spread effects and the continuous 24/7 trading schedule of Bitcoin (unlike equity markets, which follow business hours) introduce autocorrelation patterns unrelated to fundamental macroeconomic conditions. This frequency choice is consistent with recent studies in the same area, including Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022), who use weekly or higher-frequency data to estimate monetary policy effects on Bitcoin. Monthly data would reduce noise further but would produce an insufficient number of post-ETF observations, approximately 26 monthly observations following ETF approval, to estimate the post-ETF sub-model with adequate statistical power. A monthly robustness replication using n=137 observations and HAC maxlags of 3 is therefore reported alongside the primary weekly results as a supplementary specification.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2 Variable Construction
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The dependent variable, R_BTC, is the weekly log return of Bitcoin, computed as the natural logarithm of the ratio of the Friday-close price in week t to the Friday-close price in week t-1:

    R_BTC_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1})

Log returns are standard in financial econometrics for their continuous-compounding interpretability and for their tendency to produce approximate stationarity in an otherwise trending price series. Over the full 598-week sample, R_BTC has a mean of 0.0086, a standard deviation of 0.0917, and an excess kurtosis of 3.10, confirming the leptokurtic distribution typical of financial return data. Table 2.1 presents descriptive statistics for all variables in the model. Figure 2.1 plots the full time series of weekly Bitcoin log returns, with halving dates and the post-ETF period highlighted.

The macroeconomic explanatory variables are constructed as first differences of their underlying level series to ensure stationarity, consistent with the variable definitions in Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022). The change in the effective federal funds rate, dFedRate, is the weekly first difference of the federal funds rate measured in percentage points. This construction captures active monetary policy tightening or easing signals at weekly resolution. The change in the Fed balance sheet, dFedBS, is the weekly log return of total Federal Reserve assets, computed as ln(WALCL_t / WALCL_{t-1}). Log differences are preferred over simple first differences for balance sheet changes to ensure proportional interpretation of expansion and contraction episodes of different magnitudes, following the approach in Zhao et al. (2023).

Two binary dummy variables define the regime structure of the model. The PostETF dummy takes a value of 1 for all observations on or after 10 January 2024, the week of the SEC spot ETF approval, and 0 otherwise. This breakpoint is supported by price discovery evidence showing that spot ETFs became the dominant price formation venue from that date onward (Mohamad, 2025; Kia et al., 2025). The HalvingWindow dummy takes a value of 1 for any week falling within the 18-month window immediately following a Bitcoin halving event and 0 otherwise. The three halving dates within the sample period are 9 July 2016, 11 May 2020, and 19 April 2024, the November 2012 halving predates the sample and is excluded. The 18-month window is selected on the basis of the halving literature, which identifies a period of roughly 12 to 18 months as the interval over which post-halving price appreciation has historically been concentrated (Lashkaripour, 2024; Fabus et al., 2024), and represents the supply-shock absorption period described in those studies. Figure 2.2 visualises the HalvingWindow and PostETF regime periods overlaid on Bitcoin returns.

[Figure 2.2 — BTC Returns with HalvingWindow and PostETF Periods. Source: Author's calculations.]

Three interaction terms extend the baseline specification to test regime-dependent relationships. FedRate_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of dFedRate and PostETF. FedBS_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of dFedBS and PostETF. Halving_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of HalvingWindow and PostETF. These terms allow the association between each macroeconomic variable and Bitcoin returns to differ between the pre-ETF and post-ETF periods, directly testing H1 and H2.

Three control variables are included to absorb common variation in financial markets unrelated to the macro channels of interest. The S&P 500 weekly log return, R_SP500, is computed as ln(P_t / P_{t-1}) on the S&P 500 Friday-close index level and controls for broad equity market risk sentiment. Including this control ensures that any detected change in macro coefficient magnitude in the post-ETF period is not simply a reflection of increased equity co-movement. The first difference of the VIX index, dVIX = VIX_t - VIX_{t-1}, controls for week-to-week changes in aggregate investor risk aversion, consistent with the VIX variable used in Choi and Shin (2022) and Hernandez et al. (2023). The one-period lagged Bitcoin log return, R_BTC_lag1, controls for first-order autocorrelation in the dependent variable and any momentum effects documented in the crypto return literature (Liu et al., 2022). Figure 2.3 presents the 52-week rolling correlation between R_BTC and R_SP500, illustrating the time-varying equity co-movement that motivates this control.

[Figure 2.3 — Rolling 52-Week Correlation: R_BTC vs R_SP500. Source: Author's calculations.]

For the M2 robustness specification, the log growth rate of the M2 money stock, dM2 = ln(M2_t / M2_{t-1}), is added as an additional control variable. M2 captures broad monetary liquidity conditions beyond the Fed balance sheet and has been shown to co-move with Bitcoin prices at medium-term horizons (Sarker and Wang, 2022). Because M2SL is reported at monthly frequency and forward-filled to weekly observations, dM2 is non-zero only when a new monthly observation enters the weekly series.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3 Model Specification
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The primary empirical specification is ordinary least squares regression estimated over the full 598-week sample. Model 1 is the full-sample interaction model:

    R_BTC_t = alpha
              + beta_1 * dFedRate_t
              + beta_2 * dFedBS_t
              + beta_3 * HalvingWindow_t
              + beta_4 * PostETF_t
              + beta_5 * (dFedRate_t x PostETF_t)
              + beta_6 * (dFedBS_t x PostETF_t)
              + beta_7 * (HalvingWindow_t x PostETF_t)
              + gamma_1 * R_SP500_t
              + gamma_2 * R_BTC_lag1
              + gamma_3 * dVIX_t
              + epsilon_t                                           (1)

The baseline macroeconomic coefficients beta_1 and beta_2 capture the association between monetary policy changes and Bitcoin log returns in the pre-ETF period (when PostETF = 0). Based on the literature reviewed in Chapter 1, beta_1 is expected to be negative because rate tightening suppresses risk assets, including Bitcoin (Karau, 2023; Ma et al., 2022). The sign of beta_2 is expected to be positive, reflecting that Fed balance sheet expansion has historically been associated with higher Bitcoin prices through the liquidity channel (Zhao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021). The baseline halving coefficient beta_3 is expected to be positive, capturing the historically documented post-halving price appreciation (Fabus et al., 2024; Lashkaripour, 2024). The PostETF level dummy beta_4 captures any shift in the average weekly Bitcoin return in the post-ETF period net of macro conditions, without directional priors.

The interaction coefficients are the primary objects of inference for the two hypotheses. H1 is tested via beta_5 and beta_6: if the ETF approval strengthened macroeconomic sensitivity, beta_5 is expected to be negative (amplifying the tightening effect) and beta_6 is expected to be positive (amplifying the liquidity expansion effect) relative to their pre-ETF baseline values. H2 is tested via beta_7: if the halving effect has weakened in the institutional era, beta_7 is expected to be negative, such that the total post-ETF halving effect, equal to beta_3 + beta_7, is smaller in magnitude or statistically insignificant relative to the baseline beta_3.

For robustness, Model 2 estimates the baseline specification, without interaction terms and the PostETF dummy, separately on the pre-ETF sub-sample (n=484) and the post-ETF sub-sample (n=114), allowing all slope coefficients to differ freely across the two regimes without imposing a parametric interaction structure. The sub-sample coefficient estimates are compared directionally: larger magnitude or increased significance of dFedRate and dFedBS in the post-ETF period is consistent with H1, and smaller magnitude or reduced significance of HalvingWindow in the post-ETF period is consistent with H2. Because the post-ETF sub-sample contains only 114 weekly observations, Model 2 is treated as a directional robustness check rather than a primary inferential test. A monthly frequency replication of Model 1, based on n=137 monthly observations with HAC maxlags of 3, is reported to assess whether findings are sensitive to the choice of sampling frequency.

Two further robustness checks are conducted. First, an M2 liquidity robustness specification adds dM2 as a twelfth regressor to Model 1, yielding a 587-observation sample (slightly reduced due to M2 data availability). This test addresses whether the inclusion of a broader monetary aggregate, central to the thesis narrative on liquidity-driven Bitcoin returns (Zhao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021; Sarker and Wang, 2022), changes the coefficient estimates on the primary macro and halving variables. If the core interaction coefficients remain stable after controlling for M2 growth, the main results are robust to the omission of this variable from the primary specification.

Second, a multi-cryptocurrency panel robustness specification extends Model 1 to a panel of five major cryptocurrencies: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Ripple, Litecoin, and Binance Coin. The panel is constructed in long format, with the dependent variable R_crypto representing each cryptocurrency's weekly log return. The macro regressors and interaction terms from Model 1 are applied identically across all five assets, with HalvingWindow retaining Bitcoin's halving dates for all cryptocurrencies, reflecting the well-documented tendency of the broader cryptocurrency market to follow Bitcoin's supply-cycle dynamics (Elsayed and Sousa, 2022). The PostETF dummy is set at 10 January 2024 for all assets, corresponding to the BTC spot ETF approval. Each cryptocurrency's own lagged return replaces R_BTC_lag1, and four entity dummy variables capture cryptocurrency-specific fixed effects relative to Bitcoin as the reference category (Wooldridge, 2010). The resulting unbalanced panel has 2,496 weekly observations, with Bitcoin and Litecoin contributing 597 observations each and the remaining three assets contributing 434 observations each. This panel specification provides substantially greater statistical power than the Bitcoin-only model and tests whether the halving premium and its post-ETF weakening are specific to Bitcoin or common across major cryptocurrencies.

OLS is appropriate for this specification because the dependent variable is a stationary log return series, the model is linear in the parameters, and interaction terms with binary dummies are the standard analytical device for testing regime-dependent slope coefficients in financial econometrics (Karau, 2023; Ma et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2025). The stationarity of all continuous regressors is verified with Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests as documented in the following section.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 Diagnostic Tests and Estimation Method
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A battery of eight diagnostic tests is conducted to validate the estimation strategy and justify the choice of standard error correction. The results establish the conditions under which OLS with HAC standard errors is the appropriate estimator for this data.

Stationarity is assessed using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test for all continuous regressors. The binary dummy variables, PostETF and HalvingWindow, are fixed-design bounded deterministic functions that do not require stationarity testing. Among the continuous variables, R_BTC, R_SP500, and dVIX are confirmed stationary at the 1% significance level, with ADF statistics of -22.71, -26.88, and -10.65, respectively. The Fed balance sheet log change dFedBS is stationary at the 1% level with an ADF statistic of -4.69. The federal funds rate change dFedRate yields an ADF statistic of -2.60, which falls marginally above the 5% critical value (ADF = -2.86) but below the 10% critical value, its inclusion is treated as appropriate given that the series consists predominantly of zero-valued weeks, reflecting extended periods of no Federal Reserve action, punctuated by discrete tightening or easing steps, and its construction as a first difference is consistent with the specifications of Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022). The interaction terms constructed from continuous variables, FedRate_x_PostETF and FedBS_x_PostETF, are stationary at the 1% level, confirming no unit root concern from the product construction. Full ADF results are presented in Table 2.2.

[Table 2.2 — Augmented Dickey-Fuller Unit Root Test Results. Source: results/adf_results.csv]

Heteroskedasticity is assessed using the Breusch-Pagan Lagrange multiplier test. The test yields a statistic of 35.59 with a p-value of 0.0001, which decisively rejects the null hypothesis of homoskedastic residuals. This result is not surprising for Bitcoin return data, where volatility tends to cluster around major news events, policy announcements, and market stress episodes. It directly justifies the use of HAC standard errors instead of conventional homoskedastic OLS standard errors. All reported standard errors and test statistics across Model 1 and Model 2 are therefore computed using the Newey-West HAC estimator with a Bartlett kernel and a maximum lag of 10 weeks, as originally proposed by Newey and West (1987). This estimator is robust to both arbitrary heteroskedasticity and serial correlation up to the specified lag order, addressing the estimation concerns documented by White (1980) and Newey and West (1987). The choice of maxlags=10 for weekly data is consistent with the rule-of-thumb corresponding to one quarter of a year of autocovariance, sufficient to capture residual autocorrelation arising from macro release cycles.

Multicollinearity is evaluated using variance inflation factors (VIF). The maximum VIF across all regressors is 3.57, observed for the Halving_x_PostETF interaction term. This modestly elevated value is structurally expected given that the April 2024 Bitcoin halving occurred only three months after the January 2024 ETF approval: during the period from April 2024 to approximately October 2025, both HalvingWindow and PostETF equal 1 simultaneously, creating a subset of the sample where the interaction term is nearly collinear with HalvingWindow alone. Nevertheless, the VIF of 3.57 falls well below the conventional critical threshold of 10, and no other regressor has a VIF above 3.05, indicating that multicollinearity does not meaningfully inflate standard errors or distort coefficient estimates. VIF results for all variables are presented in Table 2.3.

[Table 2.3 — Variance Inflation Factors for All Regressors. Source: results/vif_results.csv]

Serial correlation in the residuals is assessed using the Durbin-Watson statistic, which equals 2.02, effectively at the value of 2 that indicates the absence of first-order autocorrelation. This result does not suggest a problem with the specification, but the use of HAC standard errors with maxlags=10 still provides protection against any higher-order serial dependence that the Durbin-Watson test does not capture.

Residual normality is examined using the Jarque-Bera test, which yields a statistic of 166.07 with a p-value of 8.66 x 10^{-37}, decisively rejecting the null hypothesis of normally distributed residuals. Non-normal residuals are a well-known feature of financial return data, reflecting the fat tails and occasional large-magnitude observations present in asset prices. This result does not invalidate OLS inference: by the central limit theorem, coefficient estimates remain consistent and asymptotically normally distributed in a sample of 598 observations, and HAC-robust standard errors further protect inference against distributional departures. The non-normality finding reinforces the importance of using robust rather than conventional standard errors, and the sample size is large enough for the asymptotic results to apply.

Structural stability at the proposed ETF breakpoint is tested using the Chow test, as proposed by Chow (1960). The Chow statistic compares the residual sum of squares from two separate sub-sample OLS regressions against the restricted residual sum of squares from the full-sample model, yielding an F-statistic under the null hypothesis that the regression coefficients are identical across the two sub-samples. Following Bai and Perron (1998), who provide a formal framework for testing multiple structural changes in linear models, two candidate breakpoints are evaluated: the primary breakpoint of 10 January 2024 (the SEC spot ETF approval date) and an alternative breakpoint of 23 October 2023, which corresponds to the resolution of the Grayscale SEC lawsuit and the DTCC disclosure of BlackRock's ETF trust, events cited by some studies as the effective informational start of the institutional transition. The Chow test results for both candidate dates are presented in Table 2.4 and discussed in detail in Chapter 3.

[Table 2.4 — Chow Structural Break Test Results. Source: results/chow_test.csv]

Finally, functional form adequacy is assessed using the Ramsey RESET test (Ramsey, 1969), which tests whether squared and cubed fitted values from Model 1 have significant joint explanatory power when added to the regression. The test yields an F-statistic of 0.754 with a p-value of 0.471, failing to reject the null hypothesis of no functional form misspecification. This result supports the appropriateness of the linear interaction specification in Model 1 and provides no evidence that the true relationship between Bitcoin returns and the explanatory variables requires higher-order polynomial terms.

Taken together, the diagnostic results establish that OLS estimation with Newey-West HAC standard errors, maxlags=10, is the appropriate and fully justified estimation procedure for this research design. The confirmed stationarity of continuous regressors, the rejection of homoskedasticity motivating HAC correction, the absence of problematic multicollinearity (maximum VIF = 3.57), the absence of first-order serial correlation (DW = 2.02), and the RESET test pass collectively validate the model structure before examining the regression results in Chapter 3.

Section 3.2 presents the sub-sample comparison (Model 2). Section 3.3 discusses the Chow structural break test. Section 3.4 reports four robustness checks: monthly frequency replication, M2 growth control, and the multi-cryptocurrency panel specification.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 Primary Model Results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The full-sample interaction model (Model 1) is estimated over 598 weekly observations from October 2014 to March 2026 using OLS with Newey-West HAC standard errors (maxlags=10). The model achieves an R-squared of 0.073 and an adjusted R-squared of 0.057. The overall F-statistic of 3.674 (p < 0.001) confirms that the regressors are jointly significant, although the low R-squared indicates that the macro and halving variables explain only a small fraction of weekly Bitcoin return variance. This is expected and consistent with the financial econometrics literature, weekly asset returns are dominated by idiosyncratic noise, and R-squared values below 0.10 are typical for macro-factor return regressions at high frequency.

[Table 3.1 — Model 1 Full-Sample Interaction Regression Results (n=598). Source: results/model1_results.txt]

Among the baseline coefficients, the change in the federal funds rate, dFedRate, is statistically significant at the 5% level with a coefficient of -0.160 (p = 0.016). This indicates that in the pre-ETF period, a one percentage point increase in the federal funds rate is associated with a 16.0 basis point decline in weekly Bitcoin log returns, consistent with the monetary tightening channel documented by Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022). The baseline halving window coefficient, HalvingWindow, is positive and statistically significant at the 1% level with a coefficient of +0.030 (p = 0.007). This means that during the 18-month post-halving windows in the pre-ETF period, Bitcoin earned an additional 3.0 percentage points per week on average relative to non-halving periods, net of macro controls. The Fed balance sheet variable, dFedBS, is not statistically significant (coefficient = -0.003, p = 0.424), suggesting that weekly fluctuations in the balance sheet do not have a detectable linear association with Bitcoin returns at this frequency. Among the control variables, the S&P 500 return (+0.326, p = 0.235), the lagged Bitcoin return (+0.035, p = 0.394), and the change in the VIX (-0.003, p = 0.099) are either insignificant or only marginally significant.

The three interaction terms, the primary objects of inference, are all statistically insignificant at the 5% and 10% significance levels. FedRate_x_PostETF has a coefficient of +0.090 (p = 0.479), indicating no detectable change in the federal funds rate sensitivity after the ETF approval. FedBS_x_PostETF has a coefficient of -0.025 (p = 0.174), directionally consistent with increased balance sheet sensitivity post-ETF but statistically indistinguishable from zero. Halving_x_PostETF has a coefficient of -0.028 (p = 0.104), which falls marginally outside the conventional 10% significance threshold. The total post-ETF halving effect, computed as the sum of the baseline and interaction coefficients (+0.030 + (-0.028) = +0.002), is economically negligible, the pre-ETF weekly halving premium of 3.0 percentage points collapses to approximately 0.2 percentage points in the post-ETF period.

Figure 3.1 displays the Model 1 residuals over time alongside their distribution, confirming the absence of systematic patterns and the leptokurtic shape typical of financial return data.

[Figure 3.1 — Model 1 Residuals Over Time and Residual Distribution. Source: Author's calculations.]

These results provide a preliminary assessment of the two hypotheses. H1 is not supported: neither interaction term on the macroeconomic variables reaches statistical significance, and the direction of the FedRate interaction (positive, implying attenuation rather than amplification) is opposite to the hypothesised sign. H2 receives directional support: the economic magnitude of the halving coefficient attenuation is large, but the statistical confidence at the weekly frequency falls short of the conventional threshold.

3.2 Sub-Sample Results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Model 2 estimates the baseline specification, without interaction terms, separately on the pre-ETF sub-sample (n = 484, October 2014 to January 2024) and the post-ETF sub-sample (n = 114, January 2024 to March 2026). The results are presented side by side to allow direct comparison of coefficient magnitudes and significance across regimes.

[Table 3.2 — Model 2 Sub-Sample Regression Results: Pre-ETF (n=484) and Post-ETF (n=114). Source: results/model2_results.txt]

In the pre-ETF sub-sample, the coefficient estimates closely mirror the full-sample baseline: dFedRate is -0.161 (p = 0.015), HalvingWindow is +0.030 (p = 0.007), and the model R-squared is 0.073. In the post-ETF sub-sample, all macro and halving coefficients lose statistical significance. The federal funds rate coefficient drops from -0.161 to -0.063 (p = 0.535), and the halving window coefficient collapses from +0.030 to +0.002 (p = 0.889). The Fed balance sheet coefficient shifts from -0.003 (p = 0.403) to -0.024 (p = 0.182), a directional increase in magnitude that is consistent with H1 but remains statistically insignificant. Notably, the S&P 500 return coefficient increases from +0.316 to +0.361, though it does not reach significance in either sub-sample.

The R-squared values are nearly identical across the two sub-samples (0.073 pre-ETF versus 0.078 post-ETF), suggesting that the overall explanatory power of the macro-halving specification has not changed much with the ETF regime shift. The sub-sample results are consistent with Model 1: macro sensitivity did not detectably increase post-ETF, and the halving premium disappeared. However, the post-ETF sub-sample contains only 114 weekly observations, which severely limits statistical power. The insignificant post-ETF results may partly reflect insufficient sample size rather than a true absence of effects, and the sub-sample comparison is therefore treated as directional evidence rather than a definitive test.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.3 Structural Break Test
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Chow structural break test evaluates whether the regression coefficients differ significantly between the pre-ETF and post-ETF sub-samples under the null hypothesis that all slope parameters are identical across the two periods. The test is conducted at two candidate breakpoint dates.

[Table 3.3 — Chow Structural Break Test Results. Source: results/chow_test.txt]

At the primary breakpoint of 10 January 2024, the Chow F-statistic is 0.951 with a p-value of 0.467, failing to reject the null hypothesis of parameter stability at any conventional significance level. At the alternative breakpoint of 23 October 2023, corresponding to the Grayscale SEC lawsuit resolution and the DTCC disclosure of BlackRock's ETF trust, the F-statistic is 1.118 with a p-value of 0.350, also failing to reject the null.

The absence of a statistically significant Chow break at either candidate date suggests that any change in Bitcoin's relationship with macroeconomic variables and halving effects has been gradual rather than abrupt. The ETF approval may have been one event in a longer process of institutional integration that began with the growth of Bitcoin futures markets and the entry of institutional investors during the 2020-2023 period (Karau, 2023; Benigno and Rosa, 2023). This gradual-transition interpretation aligns with the literature documenting a progressive strengthening of Bitcoin-equity co-movement over the decade (Bouri et al., 2022; Wu, 2025), rather than a single discrete structural break.

3.4 Robustness Checks
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Four robustness specifications are examined to assess the sensitivity of the primary findings to sampling frequency, omitted variable bias, and cross-sectional scope.


3.4.1 Monthly Frequency Replication

The Model 1 interaction specification is re-estimated at monthly frequency using 137 monthly observations from November 2014 to March 2026, with HAC standard errors and maxlags of 3. Weekly returns and first-difference macro variables are summed within each calendar month, dummy variables are taken as the last observation in the month. The monthly model achieves an R-squared of 0.185, substantially higher than the weekly R-squared of 0.073, reflecting the reduction in high-frequency noise through temporal aggregation.

[Table 3.4 — Model 1 Monthly Frequency Robustness Results (n=137). Source: results/model1_monthly_robustness.txt]

At monthly frequency, the baseline HalvingWindow coefficient is +0.135 (p = 0.016), confirming the significance of the pre-ETF halving premium at a higher aggregation level. The Halving_x_PostETF interaction is -0.153 (p = 0.068), which approaches significance at the 10% level. This provides stronger directional evidence for H2 than the weekly specification, where the same interaction reached only p = 0.104. The total post-ETF halving effect at monthly frequency is +0.135 + (-0.153) = -0.018, indicating a reversal from a positive to a slightly negative halving premium, consistent with the complete disappearance of the halving effect observed in the weekly sub-sample analysis.

For H1, the FedBS_x_PostETF interaction is -0.085 (p = 0.093) at monthly frequency, reaching marginal significance at the 10% level. This suggests that the Fed balance sheet channel may have strengthened in the post-ETF period, but the effect is not detectable at weekly frequency due to the measurement noise in weekly balance sheet fluctuations. The FedRate_x_PostETF interaction remains insignificant (-0.145, p = 0.410) at monthly frequency.


3.4.2 M2 Growth Control

The M2 robustness specification adds the log growth rate of the M2 money stock, dM2, as an additional control variable to the Model 1 interaction specification. This test addresses a potential omitted variable concern: the thesis discusses the liquidity channel literature (Zhao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021; Sarker and Wang, 2022), but the primary specification only includes the Fed balance sheet as a liquidity proxy. The M2 specification is estimated over 587 weekly observations (slightly reduced due to M2 data availability) with HAC standard errors (maxlags=10).

[Table 3.5 — Model 1 with M2 Growth Control (n=587). Source: results/model1_m2_robustness.txt]

The M2 growth variable has a positive coefficient of +1.133 and is marginally significant (p = 0.058), consistent with the idea that expansionary monetary conditions, as captured by broad money growth, are positively associated with Bitcoin returns. The sign and marginal significance are in line with the liquidity channel documented by Zhao et al. (2023) and with the M2-Bitcoin co-movement identified by Sarker and Wang (2022).

Importantly, the inclusion of dM2 does not change the core results. The dFedRate baseline coefficient moves from -0.160 to -0.158 (p = 0.015), the HalvingWindow baseline from +0.030 to +0.029 (p = 0.008), and all three interaction terms remain statistically insignificant with minimal changes in magnitude. The R-squared increases marginally from 0.073 to 0.076. These results confirm that the main findings are robust to the inclusion of M2 growth as an additional liquidity control and that leaving M2 out of the primary specification does not introduce material bias into the interaction coefficient estimates.

The significance of the halving interaction at the 1% level, achieved through the fourfold increase in sample size, shows that the weakening of the halving premium in the post-ETF period is not just a Bitcoin-specific result but a market-wide phenomenon.

================================================================================
CHAPTER 4. DISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

This chapter discusses the empirical findings in relation to the existing literature, identifies the mechanisms that may explain the observed patterns, and acknowledges the limitations of the research design.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 Discussion of Findings
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The central finding of this study is that the spot Bitcoin ETF approval in January 2024 did not produce a statistically detectable increase in Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic variables at weekly frequency, but it coincided with the disappearance of the historically significant halving premium across the cryptocurrency market. This asymmetric outcome, H1 not supported and H2 supported, was not the pattern anticipated by the hypotheses as originally framed, and it requires careful interpretation.

The non-rejection of the null for H1 can be explained in several ways. First, the post-ETF sample window contains only 114 weekly observations, which may not provide enough statistical power to detect moderate changes in regression slope coefficients through interaction terms. The marginal significance of FedBS_x_PostETF at monthly frequency (p = 0.093) suggests that a balance sheet sensitivity channel may exist but is hidden by the noise in weekly data. As the post-ETF sample grows over time, future replications with longer samples may resolve this question.

Second, the literature shows that Bitcoin's macro sensitivity began strengthening well before the ETF approval. Karau (2023) identifies the shift as occurring around 2020, and Benigno and Rosa (2023) formally date the end of the "Bitcoin-macro disconnect" to approximately the same period. If the macro integration process was already largely complete by January 2024, the ETF approval may have contributed an incremental rather than transformative change, a difference too small to detect with the available post-ETF data. The Chow test's failure to identify a structural break at either the January 2024 or October 2023 candidate dates (p = 0.467 and p = 0.350, respectively) is consistent with this gradual-transition interpretation.

Third, the post-ETF period has been characterised by substantial retail and momentum-driven trading activity in the cryptocurrency market, which may introduce noise that dilutes the macro signal. If institutional and retail flows move in opposite directions in response to macro conditions, the net effect on regression coefficients may be weakened even as the underlying institutional sensitivity increases.

The strong support for H2, particularly from the panel robustness specification (Halving_x_PostETF = -0.037, p = 0.001), aligns with multiple strands of the literature. The market efficiency interpretation of Yi et al. (2023) predicts that as the Bitcoin market matures, publicly known supply-side events should be increasingly priced in before they occur, reducing the measurable post-event return response. The attention-driven interpretation of Liu et al. (2025) and Subawa et al. (2025) suggests that halving effects work primarily through sentiment rather than supply fundamentals, and should therefore fade as institutional participants, who are less susceptible to narrative-driven trading, come to dominate the market. The direct empirical precedent is Veloso et al. (2025), who show that the 2024 halving produced the weakest price response across all four cycles. This study extends that finding by showing, for the first time in a regression framework, that the halving coefficient is not just smaller but statistically indistinguishable from zero in the post-ETF period, and that this weakening is not limited to Bitcoin but extends across major cryptocurrencies.

The panel result is particularly informative because it addresses the statistical power limitation of the Bitcoin-only analysis. With 2,496 observations across five cryptocurrencies, the panel produces a highly significant interaction (p = 0.001) where the Bitcoin-only weekly model yielded only p = 0.104. The consistency of the halving effect and its post-ETF weakening across assets with different market capitalisations, use cases, and investor bases strengthens the conclusion that this reflects a market-wide structural shift rather than something specific to Bitcoin.

The M2 robustness check provides further context for the thesis narrative. The marginally significant positive coefficient on dM2 (+1.133, p = 0.058) is consistent with the liquidity channel documented by Zhao et al. (2023) and Sarker and Wang (2022): monetary expansion, as captured by broad money growth, is positively associated with Bitcoin returns. The stability of all core coefficients after controlling for M2 confirms that the main results are not driven by omitted liquidity effects.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.2 Limitations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several limitations qualify the interpretation of the results and should be considered when assessing the generalisability of the findings.

The most fundamental limitation concerns the identification of the halving interaction effect. There are exactly three Bitcoin halvings within the sample period, July 2016, May 2020, and April 2024, and the Halving_x_PostETF interaction coefficient is identified exclusively from the third of these events, which is the only halving occurring in the post-ETF period. The regression coefficient beta_7 therefore compares one post-ETF halving cycle against two pre-ETF halving cycles. While this is an inherent feature of the available data rather than a modelling error, it means that the finding of a diminished halving effect is identified from a single post-ETF event and cannot be verified against additional post-ETF halvings until approximately 2028.

The short post-ETF observation window compounds this concern. The 114 weekly post-ETF observations (approximately 27 months of data) provide limited statistical power for detecting moderate changes in regression slope coefficients through interaction terms. The consistently insignificant H1 interaction terms may reflect a true absence of effect, or they may reflect the inability of the sample to detect a genuine but moderate increase in macro sensitivity. The monthly robustness results, which show marginal significance for the balance sheet interaction (p = 0.093), suggest that the latter interpretation cannot be ruled out.

The temporal overlap between the PostETF and HalvingWindow dummy variables creates an inherent identification challenge. The April 2024 Bitcoin halving occurred only three months after the January 2024 ETF approval, meaning that the fourth halving falls entirely within the PostETF = 1 period. This produces a VIF of 3.57 for the Halving_x_PostETF interaction, below the conventional critical threshold of 10 but sufficient to increase the standard error of the interaction coefficient and reduce statistical power. The collinearity is structural: it reflects the historical coincidence of two events and cannot be resolved through alternative model specifications.

The panel robustness specification uses Bitcoin's halving dates to define the HalvingWindow dummy for all five cryptocurrencies. This assumption is justified by the documented tendency of the broader cryptocurrency market to follow Bitcoin's supply-cycle dynamics, but it introduces the possibility that the panel results reflect Bitcoin's influence on altcoin returns rather than an independent altcoin response to the halving mechanism. The entity fixed effects control for cryptocurrency-specific average return levels but do not address cross-sectional differences in halving sensitivity.

The models do not include variables for Bitcoin-specific structural factors such as network hash rate, miner revenue, on-chain transaction volume, or stablecoin market capitalisation. These omitted variables may act as confounders, particularly around halving dates when miner capitulation events can cause independent price shocks unrelated to the macro variables in the model. The Ramsey RESET test provides no evidence of functional form misspecification (p = 0.471), but the linear interaction specification may not capture threshold or regime-switching dynamics in the Bitcoin-macro relationship. Markov-switching or threshold regression models, as used by Buthelezi (2025) and Aloui et al. (2024), represent alternative specifications that future research could apply to the same dataset.

Finally, the non-normal distribution of residuals (Jarque-Bera p < 0.001) is an expected feature of financial return data and does not invalidate OLS inference in the full 598-observation sample, where the central limit theorem ensures asymptotic normality of coefficient estimates. However, for the 114-observation post-ETF sub-sample, finite-sample inference may be imprecise, and confidence intervals should be interpreted with caution.


================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This study examined whether the approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024 constitutes a structural break in Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic factors and whether it has altered the explanatory power of the Bitcoin halving cycle for cryptocurrency returns. Two hypotheses were tested using ordinary least squares regression with Newey-West HAC standard errors on 598 weekly observations spanning July 2014 to March 2026.

The first hypothesis, that macroeconomic variables have a stronger statistical association with weekly Bitcoin returns in the post-ETF period than in the pre-ETF period, is not supported at the 5% significance level in the primary weekly specification. The interaction terms between the PostETF dummy and the federal funds rate change and the Fed balance sheet change are both statistically insignificant. The Chow structural break test fails to identify a discrete break at the ETF approval date (p = 0.467). Monthly robustness analysis provides marginal evidence for a strengthened balance sheet channel (p = 0.093), suggesting that the effect may exist but is not detectable at weekly frequency with the currently available post-ETF sample.

The second hypothesis, that the halving premium has diminished in the post-ETF institutional era, is supported. The pre-ETF halving window coefficient is positive and highly significant (+0.030, p = 0.007 in the Bitcoin-only model; +0.044, p < 0.001 in the five-cryptocurrency panel), while the total post-ETF halving effect collapses to near zero across all specifications. The multi-cryptocurrency panel robustness check produces the strongest evidence: the halving interaction coefficient is -0.037 (p = 0.001), significant at the 1% level, confirming that the weakening of the halving premium is not specific to Bitcoin but extends across major cryptocurrencies. This finding is consistent with the market efficiency hypothesis (Yi et al., 2023), the attention-based interpretation of halving effects (Liu et al., 2025), and the direct empirical observation that the 2024 halving produced the weakest price response of any cycle (Veloso et al., 2025).

The main contribution of this thesis is a unified empirical test that embeds both the January 2024 ETF structural dummy and the halving-window interaction within a single weekly-frequency regression framework spanning the full 2014-2026 period. The results suggest that institutional adoption through the ETF did not amplify macro sensitivity through the channels tested, but it coincided with a fundamental change in the role of the halving mechanism. The halving cycle, which has historically been one of the most reliable predictors of positive Bitcoin returns, no longer has a statistically significant effect in the post-institutional market.

For practitioners and investors, the practical implication is that extrapolating prior halving-cycle return patterns into the post-ETF era may be unreliable. As the Bitcoin market deepens and the share of institutionally mediated exposure grows, predictable supply-side events are likely to be increasingly priced in advance, reducing the scope for systematic post-halving return capture.

Several directions for future research emerge from this study. First, the most binding constraint on the analysis is the short post-ETF sample. Replication with data extending through the next halving cycle (expected in 2028) would provide a critical test of whether the halving weakening is permanent or specific to the 2024 cycle. Second, regime-switching or threshold regression models could capture nonlinear dynamics in the Bitcoin-macro relationship that the linear interaction specification may miss. Third, the inclusion of Bitcoin-specific on-chain variables, such as hash rate, miner outflows, and exchange reserve balances, would allow a more precise decomposition of halving effects into supply-fundamental and attention-driven components.

ABSTRACT

This study examines whether the approval of spot Bitcoin exchange-traded funds (ETFs) by the United States Securities and Exchange Commission in January 2024 constitutes a structural break in Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic factors and whether it has altered the explanatory power of the Bitcoin halving cycle for cryptocurrency returns. Using ordinary least squares regression with Newey-West heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent standard errors on 598 weekly observations from July 2014 to March 2026, the study estimates a full-sample interaction model with a PostETF regime dummy and three interaction terms to test whether the coefficients on the federal funds rate change, the Federal Reserve balance sheet change, and the halving window differ between the pre-ETF and post-ETF periods. Two hypotheses are tested. H1 posits that macroeconomic sensitivity increased after the ETF approval, H2 posits that the halving premium diminished. H1 is not supported: the macro interaction terms are statistically insignificant at the weekly frequency, though monthly robustness analysis provides marginal evidence for a strengthened balance sheet channel. H2 is supported: the halving window coefficient, which is highly significant in the pre-ETF period (+0.030, p = 0.007), collapses to near zero post-ETF. A multi-cryptocurrency panel robustness check across Bitcoin, Ethereum, Ripple, Litecoin, and Binance Coin (n = 2,496) confirms the halving weakening at the 1% significance level (p = 0.001), indicating a market-wide rather than Bitcoin-specific phenomenon. The Chow structural break test does not detect a discrete break at the ETF date, consistent with a gradual institutional integration process. The results suggest that the ETF approval did not amplify Bitcoin's macro sensitivity through the channels tested but coincided with the disappearance of the halving premium in the post-institutional era.

Keywords: Bitcoin, spot ETF, macroeconomic sensitivity, halving cycle, institutional adoption, structural break
