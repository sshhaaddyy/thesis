================================================================================
INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

The global cryptocurrency market has undergone a fundamental transformation in
recent years. Bitcoin, the world's largest cryptocurrency by market
capitalisation, has evolved from a niche digital asset into a financial
instrument held by institutional investors and regulated funds. As of early
2026, Bitcoin's market capitalisation oscillates between one and two trillion
US dollars, placing it alongside major asset classes in terms of scale. The
pivotal moment in this institutional transition came on 10 January 2024, when
the United States Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) approved the first
spot Bitcoin exchange-traded funds (ETFs). This decision opened direct Bitcoin
exposure to pension funds, wealth managers, and retail investors through
regulated brokerage accounts, triggering billions of dollars in institutional
inflows within days of the approval (Mohamad, 2025; Babalos et al., 2025).

The question of whether Bitcoin is sensitive to macroeconomic conditions is not
new, but it has become substantially more contested. Early studies found that
cryptocurrency returns were largely orthogonal to traditional macroeconomic and
financial factors (Liu and Tsyvinski, 2021; Corbet et al., 2018). Subsequent
research documented a gradual convergence, particularly during and after the
COVID-19 pandemic, as institutional investors entered the market and
equity-Bitcoin correlations rose (Bouri et al., 2022; Nguyen, 2022). The
relationship between Bitcoin and monetary policy has attracted particular
attention: studies covering the post-2020 period find that Federal Reserve rate
decisions exert a statistically significant negative effect on Bitcoin prices
(Karau, 2023; Ma et al., 2022), while Federal Reserve balance sheet expansions
appear to support cryptocurrency valuations through a liquidity channel (Zhao
et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021). Nevertheless, evidence for the pre-institutional
era is mixed, and it remains unclear whether the arrival of regulated spot ETFs
represents a structural turning point in Bitcoin's macro sensitivity or simply
a continuation of an earlier trend.

Alongside macro sensitivity, Bitcoin's halving mechanism has traditionally been
a distinguishing price driver. Approximately every four years, the block reward
paid to Bitcoin miners is halved, creating a programmatic supply shock.
Historical evidence across the 2016, 2020, and 2024 halvings documents
positive post-halving returns (Fabus et al., 2024; Lashkaripour, 2024).
However, Veloso et al. (2025) compare all four halving cycles directly and
find that the 2024 event produced substantially smaller and less statistically
significant price effects than prior cycles, raising the question of whether
institutional adoption has diminished the informational content of the supply
event.

This study examines whether the approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024
constitutes a structural break in Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic
factors and whether it has altered the explanatory power of the halving cycle
for Bitcoin returns. The aim is to quantify the change in the association
between Bitcoin weekly log returns and macroeconomic variables across the
pre- and post-ETF regimes using a full-sample interaction model with a
regime-change dummy and sub-sample robustness checks.

Two hypotheses guide the empirical analysis. H1: after institutionalisation
through the spot ETF approval in January 2024, macroeconomic variables have a
stronger statistical association with weekly Bitcoin returns than in the
pre-ETF period. H2: the explanatory power of the halving cycle for Bitcoin
returns has decreased after institutionalisation, as measured by the
halving-window coefficient losing significance or magnitude in the post-ETF
regime.

To test these hypotheses, this study applies ordinary least squares (OLS)
regression with heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent (HAC)
Newey-West standard errors to 598 weekly observations spanning July 2014 to
March 2026. The primary specification is a full-sample interaction model that
includes a PostETF binary dummy and three interaction terms —
dFedRate×PostETF, dFedBS×PostETF, and HalvingWindow×PostETF — to test whether
regime-specific coefficients differ from their baseline pre-ETF values. A Chow
structural break test independently validates the ETF breakpoint date.
Sub-sample estimation and a monthly robustness replication further verify the
conclusions.

Data are sourced from Yahoo Finance (Bitcoin-USD, S&P 500, VIX) and the
Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) service (effective federal funds rate, Fed
total assets). The sample begins in July 2014, which represents the earliest
period for which reliable weekly Bitcoin price data are consistently available,
and extends through March 2026, the latest observation available at the time of
writing.

The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 1 reviews the theoretical and
empirical literature on Bitcoin as an evolving asset class, its sensitivity to
macroeconomic factors, halving effects, and the role of institutional adoption
via spot ETFs, and derives the research gap that motivates the hypotheses.
Chapter 2 describes the data sources, variable construction, model
specification, and the diagnostic tests employed to validate the estimation
strategy. Chapter 3 presents and interprets the regression results for the
primary and robustness specifications. Chapter 4 discusses the findings in
relation to existing literature, acknowledges the limitations of the study, and
draws conclusions regarding the structural role of the ETF approval in
Bitcoin's macrofinancial integration.

CHAPTER 1. THEORETICAL AND EMPIRICAL BACKGROUND
================================================================================

This chapter reviews the existing literature relevant to the research
questions and develops the theoretical foundation for the two hypotheses. The
review is organised into four substantive sections. Section 1.1 traces
Bitcoin's evolution as an asset class. Section 1.2 surveys the evidence on
Bitcoin's sensitivity to macroeconomic factors. Section 1.3 examines the
literature on Bitcoin halving effects. Section 1.4 reviews the institutional
adoption literature centred on the spot ETF approval. Section 1.5 synthesises
the reviewed evidence and formally states the research gap.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 Bitcoin as an Evolving Asset Class
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bitcoin was introduced in 2008 by the pseudonymous author Satoshi Nakamoto as
a decentralised peer-to-peer electronic cash system, with the genesis block
mined in January 2009. For much of its first decade, Bitcoin functioned
primarily as a speculative instrument with a narrow base of cryptographic
enthusiasts and early adopters, and its market was largely isolated from
mainstream financial systems. The seminal study by Corbet et al. (2018) is
representative of this early period: examining the dynamic relationships between
cryptocurrencies and traditional financial assets from 2013 to 2018, the
authors document that Bitcoin exhibited relatively low interconnectedness with
equities and gold, positioning it as a distinct asset class whose price
dynamics operated independently of conventional financial markets. Consistent
with this finding, Liu and Tsyvinski (2021), in one of the most rigorous early
assessments of cryptocurrency risk, demonstrate that Bitcoin returns had no
statistically significant exposure to standard macroeconomic or stock-market
factors across the period 2014 to 2020. Their analysis covers a wide range of
standard risk factors — including the market excess return, size, value, and
momentum premia — and finds negligible systematic exposure, concluding that
Bitcoin constituted a standalone asset whose returns were primarily explained
by its own time-series momentum.

Over the following years, this picture changed considerably. Bouri et al.
(2022), using wavelet-based decomposition of high-order co-movement moments in
the time-frequency domain, document that Bitcoin–S&P 500 co-movement was very
weak before 2018 but strengthened noticeably thereafter. Nguyen (2022),
employing a VAR(1)-GARCH(1,1) approach on data through 2022, finds that the
S&P 500 significantly influenced Bitcoin returns during the COVID-19 period,
indicating that financial stress regimes activate the equity–Bitcoin linkage in
ways that were absent in calmer periods. By 2022, the previously celebrated
diversification benefits of Bitcoin appeared to erode precisely when investors
needed them most (Bakry et al., 2021). Wu (2025), using rolling correlation
analysis, documents that Bitcoin–S&P 500 and Bitcoin–Nasdaq correlations
peaked at approximately 0.87 in 2024 following major institutional milestones,
reaching levels that place Bitcoin closer to a high-beta equity than a
diversifying alternative asset. Marti and Reimat (2025) further report that a
one percent rise in the MSCI World index was associated with a 4.8 percent
rise in Bitcoin by 2025, providing additional evidence of deep macro
integration in the most recent sub-period.

The debate over Bitcoin's market efficiency is directly relevant to
understanding this trajectory. Yi et al. (2023), examining Bitcoin's price
formation over an extended sample, find evidence of weak-form efficiency,
implying that publicly known information — including supply-side events such as
halving dates, whose timing is fixed years in advance — should be progressively
incorporated into prices ahead of the event as the market matures and
informational barriers fall. This efficiency argument provides theoretical
grounding for the hypothesis that Bitcoin's historically distinctive supply
cycle may become less informative over time. The overarching narrative running
through the literature is therefore one of transition: from an isolated,
speculative instrument to an increasingly macro-sensitive financial instrument,
and the present study's hypotheses are formulated against this backdrop.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.2 Bitcoin's Sensitivity to Macroeconomic Factors
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The baseline evidence from the pre-pandemic era is relatively clear regarding
Bitcoin's limited macro sensitivity. Lyócsa et al. (2020) study the impact of
macroeconomic news, regulation, and hacking events on Bitcoin volatility over
the period 2013 to 2019 and find that, while hacking incidents and regulatory
announcements significantly affect volatility, scheduled US macroeconomic news
announcements produce no meaningful Bitcoin price reactions. Benigno and Rosa
(2023), in a Federal Reserve Bank of New York staff report, formally document
this "Bitcoin-macro disconnect" in the pre-institutional era, establishing a
baseline against which any post-2024 findings should be compared. Their
evidence suggests that until approximately 2020, Bitcoin and macroeconomic
variables inhabited largely separate informational universes.

The evidence for the post-2020 period is substantially different. Karau (2023),
studying the relationship between monetary policy and Bitcoin in an
event-study framework around Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meetings,
finds that Bitcoin's sensitivity to Federal Reserve policy shocks increased
substantially after 2020, with surprise monetary tightening exerting a
significant negative effect on Bitcoin prices. The study represents a key
empirical precedent for the first hypothesis of the present study, as it
demonstrates that the rate sensitivity channel became active over this decade.
This conclusion aligns with Ma et al. (2022), who estimate that a one basis
point surprise tightening on FOMC days is associated with an approximately
minus 0.25 percentage point Bitcoin return — a response magnitude substantially
larger than would be expected from a pre-institutional, macro-orthogonal asset.
Buthelezi (2025), using Markov-switching GARCH methods on the period 2015 to
2023, confirms that monetary tightening decreases cryptocurrency prices across
both high- and low-volatility regimes, reinforcing the conclusion that the
interest rate channel became consistently operative across different market
states. The international dimension is examined by Elsayed and Sousa (2022),
who apply a time-varying parameter VAR to show that G4 central bank policy
rates exert spillover effects on Bitcoin, Litecoin, and Ripple, extending
the Fed-centric framework to a broader monetary policy context.

The Federal Reserve balance sheet, widely used as a proxy for financial
liquidity conditions, provides a second transmission channel. Zhao et al.
(2023), applying time-varying parameter VAR to 2017–2023 data, find that
quantitative easing exerts a sustained positive impact on Bitcoin prices via a
liquidity channel, consistent with the view that asset price inflation during
QE periods supports riskier asset classes. Gu et al. (2021) provide event-study
evidence around the Federal Reserve's March 2020 unlimited-QE announcement and
document significant positive impacts on Bitcoin and Ethereum returns, with the
timing suggesting that the liquidity announcement itself was the operative
mechanism. The IMF's empirical framework in Che et al. (2023) integrates both
channels: Fed tightening is estimated to reduce a common cryptocurrency factor
by approximately 0.15 standard deviations — an effect exceeding the equity
market response — and the authors explicitly link this amplified sensitivity to
the entry of institutional investors into cryptocurrency markets.

Regarding risk sentiment, Choi and Shin (2022) use a structural VAR to
disentangle the effects of inflation shocks from financial uncertainty shocks
on Bitcoin returns, finding that Bitcoin behaves as an inflation hedge in
response to inflationary demand shocks but responds negatively to financial
uncertainty as captured by the VIX. This asymmetric response is consistent
with the interpretation that Bitcoin functions as a speculative risk asset
rather than a safe haven, falling when investor risk aversion rises. Hernandez
et al. (2023), employing an autoregressive distributed lag framework, document
that economic policy uncertainty negatively affects Bitcoin in the short run,
further supporting the relevance of the VIX as a control variable. Lin et al.
(2025), using quantile regression on 2018–2024 data, add that the US dollar
exchange rate and Treasury yields are significant drivers of Bitcoin returns
at multiple quantiles of the return distribution, reinforcing the conclusion
that Bitcoin's macro sensitivity became multidimensional over the decade.

Despite this growing body of evidence, a notable gap persists. Most existing
studies analyse the evolution of macro sensitivity up to approximately 2023 and
do not directly test whether the specific institutional event of the January
2024 spot ETF approval constitutes a measurable amplification of these
sensitivities. The interaction model approach employed in the present study,
which directly estimates whether post-ETF macro regression coefficients differ
statistically from pre-ETF levels, is designed to address this gap.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.3 Bitcoin Halving Effects
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bitcoin's supply schedule is encoded in its protocol: approximately every
210,000 blocks, or roughly four years, the reward paid to miners for validating
transactions is halved. This mechanism creates a programmatic reduction in the
rate of new Bitcoin supply. The halvings occurred on 28 November 2012,
9 July 2016, 11 May 2020, and 19 April 2024, respectively. The supply-side
logic predicts that, holding demand constant, a reduction in the rate of new
supply creation should exert upward pressure on prices — a direct application
of supply-and-demand theory to a fixed-supply monetary asset.

Empirical evidence on halving effects is broadly supportive of a positive price
outcome, at least across the earlier cycles. Fabus et al. (2024) document the
post-halving price cycle using technical indicators and regression analysis
across the 2012, 2016, and 2020 halvings, identifying a predictable pattern of
price appreciation in the months following each supply event. Lashkaripour
(2024), using double-selection LASSO and OLS on a multi-cycle dataset, confirms
positive post-halving returns while also documenting that the magnitude of
halving sensitivity weakened across successive cycles, consistent with the
market maturation hypothesis. Liu et al. (2022), providing a comprehensive
factor model of cryptocurrency returns, offer theoretical grounding for the
diminishing pattern by showing that as Bitcoin matures, its pricing increasingly
reflects systematic risk factors rather than idiosyncratic supply-side events.

Two competing interpretations of the halving mechanism are particularly relevant
to the present study. The supply-fundamental view holds that the halving exerts
genuine fundamental price pressure via the direct reduction in miner-produced
supply entering the market. Alternatively, Liu et al. (2025) document negative
cumulative average abnormal returns around halving dates and attribute the price
dynamics to attention-driven trading behaviour rather than supply fundamentals,
suggesting that halvings produce their effects primarily through investor
sentiment and media coverage. Under this attention-driven interpretation, halving
effects should diminish in a more liquid, institutionally dominated market where
sophisticated participants can arbitrage away predictable patterns in advance.
Subawa et al. (2025) reach a similar conclusion using partial least squares
structural equation modelling, finding that halvings affect prices via sentiment
and regulatory channels, consistent with the non-fundamental mechanism. The
efficiency lens provides an integrating explanation: Yi et al. (2023) present
evidence that as Bitcoin's market deepens, halvings — whose dates are publicly
known years in advance — are increasingly pre-priced into the asset's valuation,
reducing the measurable post-event return response.

The most directly relevant evidence for the present study comes from Veloso
et al. (2025), who compare cumulative abnormal returns and volatility across all
four halving events. Their analysis finds that the 2024 halving produced
substantially smaller and less statistically significant price effects than any
of the three prior cycles, consistent with both the market maturation hypothesis
and the attention-based interpretation. This paper constitutes the strongest
direct empirical support for the second hypothesis of the present study.

Despite this evidence, no existing study has tested whether the arrival of large
institutional ETF inflows, beginning in January 2024, directly contributed to
the weakening of the 2024 halving effect. The near-simultaneous timing of the
ETF approval and the April 2024 halving creates an identification challenge
that standard single-event analyses cannot resolve. The present study uses a
halving-window interaction term with the PostETF dummy to directly assess
whether the halving coefficient differs between the pre-ETF and post-ETF
institutional regimes.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.4 Institutional Adoption and the Spot Bitcoin ETF
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The institutionalisation of Bitcoin proceeded gradually over the decade
following its introduction. The launch of Bitcoin futures on the Chicago
Mercantile Exchange in December 2017 marked the first regulated derivatives
product, enabling institutional hedging and providing a new price discovery
venue. Boan et al. (2021) document that institutional Bitcoin futures trading
influenced Bitcoin–S&P 500 correlations in the period before the spot ETF
approval, establishing a pre-institutional baseline from which the ETF's
incremental effect can be assessed. Throughout the 2017–2023 period, the
Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) offered an alternative pathway for institutional
exposure, though at the cost of structural premium and discount volatility
relative to net asset value. The SEC declined multiple spot ETF applications
during this period on grounds of insufficient market manipulation protections.

The landscape changed materially when the SEC approved eleven spot Bitcoin ETFs
on 10 January 2024, including BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) and
Fidelity's Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC). This decision was not merely
symbolic: it enabled direct, low-cost, regulated Bitcoin exposure for
institutional investors and retail savers through standard brokerage
infrastructure. Liu and Yang (2024) describe the ETF approval as an elevation
of Bitcoin's market legitimacy and liquidity depth, arguing that this mechanism
provides a structural rationale for why macro factor exposures would change
following the approval. Guliyev and Ahmadova (2025) confirm, using cointegration
analysis on January 2024 to May 2025 data, that ETF assets under management are
cointegrated with Bitcoin spot prices, indicating that institutional flows exert
a sustained and statistically significant influence on Bitcoin valuation beyond
the immediate announcement window.

Price discovery dynamics shifted markedly following the approval. Mohamad (2025)
finds that the spot ETFs — primarily IBIT, FBTC, and GBTC — dominate Bitcoin
price discovery approximately 85 percent of the time following their
introduction, with the traditional spot market playing a secondary role. Kia
et al. (2025) confirm that after 10 January 2024, the spot market overtook
futures-based ETFs as the primary venue for price formation, representing a
qualitative change in the market microstructure. These findings directly
validate the choice of 10 January 2024 as the breakpoint date for the PostETF
dummy variable in the present study, as they demonstrate that the structural
change in price formation coincides precisely with the approval date.

The consequences for Bitcoin's correlation structure with equities have been
measured by several studies. Babalos et al. (2025) document that the ETF
introduction increased Bitcoin returns and reduced volatility for both Bitcoin
and Ripple, consistent with a liquidity-driven improvement in market quality.
Wu (2025), using rolling DCC-GARCH analysis, reports that Bitcoin–equity
correlations peaked at approximately 0.87 in 2024 in the wake of institutional
milestones — a level that suggests substantial co-movement with equities and
challenges the traditional characterisation of Bitcoin as an independent asset.
Hong et al. (2025), using a Chow test at the January 2024 breakpoint alongside
DCC-GARCH estimation, find that Bitcoin's correlation with equities increased
significantly post-ETF and that its hedging effectiveness against traditional
assets changed as a result. Taken together, these studies are broadly consistent
with the view that ETF-mediated institutional entry has driven Bitcoin toward
the behaviour of a high-beta equity, implying that macroeconomic variables —
which govern equity market cycles — should increasingly explain Bitcoin returns
in the post-institutional era.

Despite this rich descriptive and event-study literature, a direct test of
whether the ETF approval amplified the magnitude of Bitcoin's regression
coefficients on standard macroeconomic variables across a continuous long-run
weekly dataset spanning both regimes remains absent in the existing literature.
The present study fills this gap by estimating an interaction OLS model over
the full 2014–2026 weekly sample, allowing the macro coefficients to shift at
the January 2024 ETF date, and testing the statistical significance of that
shift.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.5 Synthesis and Research Gap
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The review above establishes three broad empirical regularities. First, Bitcoin
has progressively transitioned from a macro-orthogonal speculative asset to one
increasingly integrated into the macrofinancial system, with the Federal Reserve
rate and liquidity channels becoming operative particularly after 2020 (Benigno
and Rosa, 2023; Karau, 2023; Che et al., 2023). Second, the supply-side halving
mechanism — which historically drove positive post-halving returns — shows signs
of diminishing relevance as Bitcoin's market matures, with the 2024 halving
producing the weakest measured price effect across all four cycles (Veloso et
al., 2025; Lashkaripour, 2024). Third, the spot ETF approval in January 2024
marks the most significant institutional inflection point in Bitcoin's history,
fundamentally reshaping price discovery (Mohamad, 2025; Kia et al., 2025) and
equity correlation structure (Wu, 2025; Hong et al., 2025). What the literature
does not yet offer, however, is a unified empirical test of whether the ETF
approval constitutes a structural breakpoint for regression coefficients on
macroeconomic variables and for the halving window premium, estimated over a
single weekly-frequency dataset spanning the full 2014–2026 period and
encompassing all four Bitcoin halvings.

This study addresses that gap directly. The contribution is a full-sample
weekly OLS interaction model that, for the first time, includes both the
January 2024 ETF structural dummy and the April 2024 halving within the same
estimation window, enabling a direct test of regime-conditional macro
sensitivities. The two hypotheses derived from the literature review are stated
formally as follows. H1: after institutionalisation through the spot ETF
approval in January 2024, macroeconomic variables have a stronger statistical
association with weekly Bitcoin returns than in the pre-ETF period. H2: the
explanatory power of the halving cycle for Bitcoin returns has decreased after
institutionalisation, as measured by the halving-window coefficient losing
significance or magnitude in the post-ETF regime.

CHAPTER 2. DATA AND METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

This chapter describes the data sources, sample period, variable construction,
model specification, and diagnostic procedures underpinning the empirical
analysis. Section 2.1 describes the data sources and sample period. Section 2.2
defines all eleven variables. Section 2.3 presents the model specifications.
Section 2.4 documents the diagnostic tests conducted to validate the
estimation approach.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 Data Sources and Sample Period
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The dataset comprises weekly Friday-close observations for five underlying
data series collected from two publicly available sources. Bitcoin price data
(ticker: BTC-USD), S&P 500 index data (ticker: ^GSPC), and the CBOE Volatility
Index (ticker: ^VIX) are obtained from Yahoo Finance. The effective federal
funds rate (FRED series: DFF) and the Federal Reserve total assets series
(FRED series: WALCL), used as a proxy for the Federal Reserve balance sheet,
are retrieved from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) service maintained
by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. All series are aligned to a
Friday-close weekly convention. For the daily FRED series (DFF), the weekly
observation is computed as the arithmetic mean of the Monday-through-Friday
daily values for the calendar week ending on that Friday. For the WALCL series,
which is released each Thursday, the Thursday observation is used as the proxy
for the week ending on the subsequent Friday.

The full sample spans July 2014 to March 2026, yielding 598 usable weekly
observations after removing the first observation, which is consumed by the
one-period lag of the Bitcoin return. The start date of July 2014 reflects the
earliest period for which reliable, continuous weekly Bitcoin price data are
consistently available from Yahoo Finance; data for earlier periods are sparse,
subject to significant gaps, and affected by the price-discovery anomalies of
the nascent exchange infrastructure that characterised 2011 to 2013. The end
date of March 2026 represents the latest weekly observation available at the
time of analysis. For the sub-sample robustness check in Model 2, the pre-ETF
period covers October 2014 through 5 January 2024 (n=484 observations) and
the post-ETF period covers 12 January 2024 through 13 March 2026 (n=114
observations).

The choice of weekly frequency is motivated by both theoretical and practical
considerations. Weekly data capture the medium-term policy-response dynamics
most relevant to the macroeconomic variables of interest while avoiding the
microstructure noise inherent in daily Bitcoin data, where bid-ask spread
effects and the continuous 24/7 trading schedule of Bitcoin (unlike equity
markets, which follow business hours) introduce autocorrelation patterns
unrelated to fundamental macroeconomic conditions. This choice of frequency
is consistent with recent studies in the same literature, including Karau (2023)
and Ma et al. (2022), who use weekly or higher-frequency data to estimate
monetary policy effects on Bitcoin. Monthly data would reduce noise further
but would produce an insufficient number of post-ETF observations — approximately
26 monthly observations following ETF approval — to estimate the post-ETF
sub-model with adequate statistical power. A monthly robustness replication
using n=137 observations and HAC maxlags of 3 is therefore reported alongside
the primary weekly results as a supplementary specification.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2 Variable Construction
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The dependent variable, R_BTC, is the weekly log return of Bitcoin, computed
as the natural logarithm of the ratio of the Friday-close price in week t to
the Friday-close price in week t−1:

    R_BTC_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1})

Log returns are standard in financial econometrics for their continuous-
compounding interpretability and for their tendency to produce approximate
stationarity in an otherwise trending price series. Over the full 598-week
sample, R_BTC has a mean of 0.0086, a standard deviation of 0.0917, and an
excess kurtosis of 3.10, confirming the leptokurtic distribution typical of
financial return data. Table 2.1 presents descriptive statistics for all
variables in the model.

[Table 2.1 — Descriptive Statistics for All Model Variables (n=598).
Source: results/descriptive_stats.csv]

The macroeconomic explanatory variables are constructed as first differences
of their underlying level series to ensure stationarity, consistent with the
variable definitions in Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022). The change in the
effective federal funds rate, dFedRate, is the weekly first difference of the
federal funds rate measured in percentage points. This construction captures
active monetary policy tightening or easing signals at weekly resolution. The
change in the Fed balance sheet, dFedBS, is the weekly log return of total
Federal Reserve assets, computed as ln(WALCL_t / WALCL_{t-1}). Log differences
are preferred over simple first differences for balance sheet changes to ensure
proportional interpretation of expansion and contraction episodes of different
magnitudes, following the approach in Zhao et al. (2023).

Two binary dummy variables define the regime structure of the model. The
PostETF dummy takes a value of 1 for all observations on or after 10 January
2024 — the week of the SEC spot ETF approval — and 0 otherwise. This breakpoint
is supported by price discovery evidence showing that spot ETFs became the
dominant price formation venue from that date onward (Mohamad, 2025; Kia et
al., 2025). The HalvingWindow dummy takes a value of 1 for any week falling
within the 18-month window immediately following a Bitcoin halving event and
0 otherwise. The three halving dates within the sample period are 9 July 2016,
11 May 2020, and 19 April 2024; the November 2012 halving predates the sample
and is excluded. The 18-month window is selected on the basis of the halving
literature, which identifies a period of roughly 12 to 18 months as the
interval over which post-halving price appreciation has historically been
concentrated (Lashkaripour, 2024; Fabus et al., 2024), and represents the
supply-shock absorption period described in those studies.

Three interaction terms extend the baseline specification to test regime-
dependent relationships. FedRate_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of
dFedRate and PostETF. FedBS_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of dFedBS
and PostETF. Halving_x_PostETF is the element-wise product of HalvingWindow
and PostETF. These terms allow the association between each macroeconomic
variable and Bitcoin returns to differ between the pre-ETF and post-ETF
periods, directly operationalising H1 and H2.

Three control variables are included to absorb common variation in financial
markets unrelated to the macro channels of interest. The S&P 500 weekly log
return, R_SP500, is computed as ln(P_t / P_{t-1}) on the S&P 500 Friday-close
index level and controls for broad equity market risk sentiment. Including this
control ensures that any detected change in macro coefficient magnitude in the
post-ETF period is not simply a reflection of increased equity co-movement.
The first difference of the VIX index, dVIX = VIX_t − VIX_{t-1}, controls for
week-to-week changes in aggregate investor risk aversion, consistent with the
VIX variable used in Choi and Shin (2022) and Hernandez et al. (2023). The
one-period lagged Bitcoin log return, R_BTC_lag1, controls for first-order
autocorrelation in the dependent variable and any momentum effects documented
in the crypto return literature (Liu et al., 2022).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3 Model Specification
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The primary empirical specification is ordinary least squares regression
estimated over the full 598-week sample. Model 1 is the full-sample interaction
model:

    R_BTC_t = alpha
              + beta_1 * dFedRate_t
              + beta_2 * dFedBS_t
              + beta_3 * HalvingWindow_t
              + beta_4 * PostETF_t
              + beta_5 * (dFedRate_t x PostETF_t)
              + beta_6 * (dFedBS_t x PostETF_t)
              + beta_7 * (HalvingWindow_t x PostETF_t)
              + gamma_1 * R_SP500_t
              + gamma_2 * R_BTC_lag1
              + gamma_3 * dVIX_t
              + epsilon_t                                           (1)

The baseline macroeconomic coefficients beta_1 and beta_2 capture the
association between monetary policy changes and Bitcoin log returns in the
pre-ETF period (i.e., when PostETF = 0). Based on the literature reviewed
in Chapter 1, beta_1 is expected to be negative: rate tightening suppresses
risk assets, including Bitcoin (Karau, 2023; Ma et al., 2022). The sign of
beta_2 is expected to be positive, reflecting that Fed balance sheet expansion
has historically been associated with higher Bitcoin prices through the
liquidity channel (Zhao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021). The baseline halving
coefficient beta_3 is expected to be positive, capturing the historically
documented post-halving price appreciation (Fabus et al., 2024; Lashkaripour,
2024). The PostETF level dummy beta_4 captures any shift in the average weekly
Bitcoin return in the post-ETF period net of macro conditions, without
directional priors.

The interaction coefficients are the primary objects of inference for the two
hypotheses. H1 is tested via beta_5 and beta_6: if the ETF approval
strengthened macroeconomic sensitivity, beta_5 is expected to be negative
(amplifying the tightening effect) and beta_6 is expected to be positive
(amplifying the liquidity expansion effect) relative to their pre-ETF baseline
values. H2 is tested via beta_7: if the halving effect has weakened in the
institutional era, beta_7 is expected to be negative, such that the total
post-ETF halving effect — equal to beta_3 + beta_7 — is smaller in magnitude
or statistically insignificant relative to the baseline beta_3.

For robustness, Model 2 estimates the baseline specification — omitting
interaction terms and the PostETF dummy — separately on the pre-ETF sub-sample
(n=484) and the post-ETF sub-sample (n=114), allowing all slope coefficients
to differ freely across the two regimes without imposing a parametric
interaction structure. The sub-sample coefficient estimates are compared
directionally: larger magnitude or increased significance of dFedRate and
dFedBS in the post-ETF period is consistent with H1, and smaller magnitude or
reduced significance of HalvingWindow in the post-ETF period is consistent with
H2. Because the post-ETF sub-sample contains only 114 weekly observations,
Model 2 is treated as a directional robustness check rather than a primary
inferential test. A monthly frequency replication of Model 1, based on n=137
monthly observations with HAC maxlags of 3, is reported to assess whether
findings are sensitive to the choice of sampling frequency.

OLS is appropriate for this specification because the dependent variable is a
stationary log return series, the model is linear in the parameters, and
interaction terms with binary dummies are the standard analytical device for
testing regime-dependent slope coefficients in financial econometrics (Karau,
2023; Ma et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2025). The stationarity of all continuous
regressors is verified with Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests as documented in the
following section.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 Diagnostic Tests and Estimation Method
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A battery of eight diagnostic tests is conducted to validate the estimation
strategy and justify the choice of standard error correction. The results
establish the conditions under which OLS with HAC standard errors is the
appropriate estimator for the data at hand.

Stationarity is assessed using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test for
all continuous regressors. The binary dummy variables — PostETF and
HalvingWindow — are fixed-design, bounded deterministic functions that do not
require stationarity testing. Among the continuous variables, R_BTC, R_SP500,
and dVIX are confirmed stationary at the 1% significance level, with ADF
statistics of −22.71, −26.88, and −10.65, respectively. The Fed balance sheet
log change dFedBS is stationary at the 1% level with an ADF statistic of −4.69.
The federal funds rate change dFedRate yields an ADF statistic of −2.60, which
falls marginally above the 5% critical value (ADF = −2.86) but below the 10%
critical value; its inclusion is treated as appropriate given that the series
consists predominantly of zero-valued weeks — reflecting extended periods of
no Federal Reserve action — punctuated by discrete tightening or easing steps,
and its construction as a first difference is consistent with the specifications
of Karau (2023) and Ma et al. (2022). The interaction terms constructed from
continuous variables — FedRate_x_PostETF and FedBS_x_PostETF — are stationary
at the 1% level, confirming no unit root concern from the product construction.
Full ADF results are presented in Table 2.2.

[Table 2.2 — Augmented Dickey-Fuller Unit Root Test Results. Source:
results/adf_results.csv]

Heteroskedasticity is assessed using the Breusch-Pagan Lagrange multiplier
test. The test yields a statistic of 35.59 with a p-value of 0.0001, decisively
rejecting the null hypothesis of homoskedastic residuals. This result is not
surprising for Bitcoin return data, where volatility clusters around major news
events, policy announcements, and market stress episodes. It directly justifies
the use of HAC standard errors in preference to conventional homoskedastic OLS
standard errors. All reported standard errors and test statistics across Model 1
and Model 2 are therefore computed using the Newey-West HAC estimator with a
Bartlett kernel and a maximum lag of 10 weeks, as originally proposed by Newey
and West (1987). This estimator is robust to both arbitrary heteroskedasticity
and serial correlation up to the specified lag order, addressing the estimation
concerns documented jointly by White (1980) and Newey and West (1987). The
choice of maxlags=10 for weekly data is consistent with the rule-of-thumb
corresponding to one quarter of a year of autocovariance, sufficient to capture
residual autocorrelation arising from macro release cycles.

Multicollinearity is evaluated using variance inflation factors (VIF). The
maximum VIF across all regressors is 3.57, observed for the Halving_x_PostETF
interaction term. This modestly elevated value is structurally expected given
that the April 2024 Bitcoin halving occurred only three months after the January
2024 ETF approval: during the period from April 2024 to approximately October
2025, both HalvingWindow and PostETF equal 1 simultaneously, creating a subset
of the sample where the interaction term is nearly collinear with HalvingWindow
alone. Nevertheless, the VIF of 3.57 falls well below the conventional critical
threshold of 10, and no other regressor exhibits a VIF above 3.05, indicating
that multicollinearity does not materially inflate standard errors or distort
coefficient estimates. VIF results for all variables are presented in Table 2.3.

[Table 2.3 — Variance Inflation Factors for All Regressors. Source:
results/vif_results.csv]

Serial correlation in the residuals is assessed using the Durbin-Watson
statistic, which equals 2.02, effectively at the value of 2 that indicates
the absence of first-order autocorrelation. This result does not indicate a
problem with the specification, but the use of HAC standard errors with
maxlags=10 nevertheless provides protection against any higher-order serial
dependence that the Durbin-Watson test does not capture.

Residual normality is examined using the Jarque-Bera test, which yields a
statistic of 166.07 with a p-value of 8.66 × 10^{-37}, decisively rejecting
the null hypothesis of normally distributed residuals. Non-normal residuals
are a well-documented feature of financial return data, reflecting the fat
tails and occasional large-magnitude observations inherent in asset prices.
This result does not invalidate OLS inference: by the central limit theorem,
coefficient estimates remain consistent and asymptotically normally distributed
in a sample of 598 observations, and HAC-robust standard errors further
insulate inference against distributional departures. The non-normality finding
reinforces the importance of using robust rather than conventional standard
errors, and the sample size is sufficiently large for the asymptotic results
to apply.

Structural stability at the proposed ETF breakpoint is tested using the Chow
test, as proposed by Chow (1960). The Chow statistic compares the residual
sum of squares from two separate sub-sample OLS regressions against the
restricted residual sum of squares from the full-sample model, yielding an
F-statistic under the null hypothesis that the regression coefficients are
identical across the two sub-samples. Following Bai and Perron (1998), who
provide a formal framework for testing multiple structural changes in linear
models, two candidate breakpoints are evaluated: the primary breakpoint of
10 January 2024 (the SEC spot ETF approval date) and an alternative breakpoint
of 23 October 2023, which corresponds to the resolution of the Grayscale SEC
lawsuit and the DTCC disclosure of BlackRock's ETF trust — events cited by
some studies as the effective informational start of the institutional
transition. The Chow test results for both candidate dates are presented in
Table 2.4 and discussed in detail in Chapter 3.

[Table 2.4 — Chow Structural Break Test Results. Source:
results/chow_test.csv]

Finally, functional form adequacy is assessed using the Ramsey RESET test
(Ramsey, 1969), which tests whether squared and cubed fitted values from
Model 1 have significant joint explanatory power when added to the regression.
The test yields an F-statistic of 0.754 with a p-value of 0.471, failing to
reject the null hypothesis of no functional form misspecification. This result
supports the appropriateness of the linear interaction specification in
Model 1 and provides no evidence that the true relationship between Bitcoin
returns and the explanatory variables requires higher-order polynomial terms.

Taken together, the diagnostic results establish that OLS estimation with
Newey-West HAC standard errors, maxlags=10, is the appropriate and fully
justified estimation procedure for the research design described in this
chapter. The confirmed stationarity of continuous regressors, the rejection
of homoskedasticity motivating HAC correction, the absence of problematic
multicollinearity (maximum VIF = 3.57), the absence of first-order serial
correlation (DW = 2.02), and the RESET test pass collectively validate the
model structure prior to examining the regression results in Chapter 3.
