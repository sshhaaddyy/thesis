COMMENTS:

1. Results interpretation: you are honest about the mixed findings, and that is good. H1 not supported at weekly frequency, H2 partial support. This is a perfectly fine outcome for a thesis, "no effect found" is a valid result, as long as you discuss why. The key is framing: the thesis is not "failed" because the hypotheses are not confirmed. It shows that the transition, if real, is gradual and not yet statistically detectable with the available post-ETF data.

2. The halving identification problem. This is the most important limitation to discuss clearly. There are exactly 3 halvings in your sample: 2016, 2020, 2024. The HalvingWindow × PostETF interaction is equal to 1 ONLY during the third halving window (Apr 2024 – Oct 2025). So β9 is effectively comparing one post-ETF halving cycle to two pre-ETF cycles. That is a sample of 3 "events." You correctly note the VIF=3.57 collinearity, but the thesis should state explicitly: the halving interaction coefficient is identified from a single event, which limits what we can generalize. This is not a flaw in your model, it is an inherent limitation of the data. Discuss it. 

3. Cross-section (multiple cryptos). I know you asked before why include other cryptocurrencies. You proceeded without them, and the BTC-only analysis tells a coherent story. However, with a single asset you have no cross-sectional variation — every observation shares the same PostETF, the same HalvingWindow, the same macro environment. A panel of even 5-10 top altcoins would add variation in macro sensitivity across different cryptos and strengthen the identification. I am not asking you to redo the main analysis. But consider adding a brief robustness section: run your Model 1 specification on a small panel (5-10 top cryptos by market cap, with crypto fixed effects). This would significantly strengthen the thesis.

4. Missing controls. Your earlier draft mentioned 4 controls: VIX, Nasdaq return, lagged BTC return, M2 growth. The final model uses only VIX and S&P 500. Consider adding as robustness checks:
   - Lagged R_BTC (one-week lag) — standard for return regressions, controls for autocorrelation.
   - M2 growth — this is central to your thesis narrative ("has BTC become a leveraged play on M2?"). If it is part of the story, it should appear somewhere in the analysis, at least as a robustness specification.

5. References. The literature list is still mostly non-academic (blog posts, industry reports, news articles). For a BA thesis you need proper academic papers. Key areas:
   - Crypto return predictability and macro factors
   - Effect of ETF introduction on underlying asset prices (there is existing literature on gold ETFs, commodity ETFs — use the analogy)
   - Bitcoin halving and supply shocks (academic treatments)
   Check the requird number of references in the requirements. This is critical.

6. R² = 0.073. You correctly note this is normal for financial return regressions. Include a brief paragraph in the methodology section explaining why: macro variables explain long-run trends, not weekly noise. Financial returns are inherently difficult to predict at high frequency. Low R² does not invalidate the analysis.
